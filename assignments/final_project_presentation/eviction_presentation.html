Predicting Eviction Crises in Philadelphia

Early Warning System Using Macroeconomic Data

Josh, Zhe Fang, Zicheng Xiang, Yanyang Chen

2025-12-08

1. The Policy Problem

What we are predicting: We are NOT predicting who or what. We are predicting WHEN.

  • Goal: Determine if eviction rates respond to economic disruptions.
  • Dependent Variable: percentage_diff (Deviation from historical baseline).
  • Context: Communities vulnerable to immediate budget stress are most affected.

2. Why This Matters

Prevention is cheaper than crisis response

  • Legal aid: $5-10k per case
  • Homelessness services: $50k+ per family
  • Lost tax revenue, community disruption

Key insight: Early warning → Early intervention

3. Our Solution

A model that uses economic analytics to provide an Early Warning System.

  • Focuses on identifying periods of heightened risk.
  • Allows policymakers to prepare resources before the surge hits.

4. Data Integration

Predictors sourced from three datasets:

  1. Eviction Lab: l1_percentage_diff (Lagged eviction pressure)
  2. BLS (Macroeconomic):
    • unemployment_rate (Economic shock)
    • cpi (Inflation/Cost of living)
  3. Urban Institute: Household financial vulnerability indicators (e.g., debt, housing burden)

5. Model Specification

Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) selected because outcome is continuous and symmetric.

Dependent Variable: percentage_diff - Measures how monthly eviction filings deviate from a historical baseline. - Focus is on modeling unexpected increases/decreases.

Predictors: - Lagged eviction pressure: l1_percentage_diff (Accounts for persistence). - Macroeconomic conditions: unemployment_rate, cpi. - Household financial vulnerability: Variables distinguishing short-term disruption from structural conditions.

6. Results

Model Performance: - R²: 0.7975 (Explains ~80% of variation)

Key Findings on Coefficients: 1. Lagged Eviction Pressure: Positive and highly significant. - Meaning: Strong persistence; once pressure rises, it tends to continue. 2. Unemployment: Statistically significant negative coefficient. - Meaning: Consistent with income shock theory regarding deviations from baseline. 3. CPI: Positive but not statistically significant. - Meaning: Short-run inflation effects are less immediate than labor market shocks.

Note on Urban Institute Data: - Variables were dropped from coefficients due to perfect collinearity (they act as baseline constants), but serve as structural context.

7. Validation Strategy

Comparison of Prediction Errors:

Metric Breakdown:

  • Left: In-Sample (Jan 2020 - Dec 2022)
    • MAE: 0.1225 | RMSE: 0.1711
    • Observation: Model accurately captures historical month-to-month deviations.
  • Right: Out-of-Sample (Jan 2023 - Present)
    • MAE: 0.1532 | RMSE: 0.1833
    • Observation: Performance remains stable when forecasting unseen economic conditions.

8. Prediction Capabilities

What the model predicts WELL:

  • Early Warnings: Signals upcoming months of high eviction stress 1-3 months in advance, enabling proactive resource deployment. Out-of-sample validation (RMSE = 0.1833) confirms stable performance on unseen economic conditions.
  • Temporal Patterns: Identifies when intensity of resources should be deployed by explaining ~80% of month-to-month variation in eviction filings (R² = 0.7975).

What the model does NOT predict well:

  • Spatial Specificity: Cannot pinpoint specific houses, addresses, or neighborhoods. This is a citywide temporal index, not a localized forecasting tool.
  • Exogenous Policy Shocks: Cannot forecast unexpected policy decisions (e.g., new moratoriums) that fall outside economic modeling.

9. Critical Limitations

Temporal vs. Spatial: - The model successfully predicts citywide temporal fluctuations (time). - It does not capture neighborhood-level variation (space) because the financial vulnerability data is static over the modeled time period.

Policy Shocks: - Cannot predict exogenous policy decisions (e.g., new moratoriums) that are not economic in nature.

10. Required Safeguards

Can’t deploy without these:

  1. Community Oversight Board - Tenant orgs, legal aid, Council members (veto power).
  2. Annual Bias Audits - Public reporting of model accuracy.
  3. Prevention-only use - Never for enforcement.
  4. Transparency - Public code, equations, predictions.

11. Implementation Roadmap

Phase 1 (Months 1-3): Form oversight board, run in shadow mode.

Phase 2 (Months 4-12): Public launch, coordinate with legal aid + rental assistance.

Phase 3 (Year 2+): Evaluate outcomes, refine.

Cost-benefit: High ROI by targeting prevention resources efficiently.

12. Recommendation

Deploy as a Monthly Index

  • Use the predictions as a Monthly Eviction Pressure Index.
  • Action: Update data monthly to monitor changes from month-to-month.
  • Resource Deployment: Use the index to determine when intensity of resources (legal aid, funding) should be scaled up.

Condition: Must be paired with the safeguards listed in Slide 10.
